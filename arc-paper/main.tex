% ABSTRACT
% ==============================================================
\begin{abstract}
We introduce \annotatexname, a novel annotation-guided reasoning framework for solving Abstract and Reasoning Corpus (ARC) tasks. Unlike pure neural approaches that lack interpretable reasoning chains, or pure symbolic methods that require exhaustive search, \annotatexname{} combines structured visual annotation with multi-strategy pattern inference to achieve robust abstract reasoning. Our framework comprises four synergistic components: a \emph{Pattern Analyzer} that extracts geometric, chromatic, and structural features from input--output grid pairs; a \emph{Grid Encoder} that produces compact symbolic representations; a \emph{Rule Inference} module that hypothesizes transformation rules across four strategy families (geometric, color-based, structural, and composite); and a \emph{Self-Consistency Decoder} that resolves ambiguities through majority voting over multiple valid hypotheses. We evaluate \annotatexname{} on the ARC-AGI-2 benchmark, demonstrating that annotation-guided reasoning achieves competitive performance while maintaining full interpretability of the reasoning chain. Ablation studies confirm that each component contributes meaningfully, with the multi-strategy ensemble providing the largest single gain. Our approach establishes a new paradigm for neuro-symbolic visual reasoning that bridges the gap between pattern recognition and programmatic rule inference.
\end{abstract}

Abstract Reasoning, ARC-AGI, Visual Reasoning, Neuro-Symbolic AI, Pattern Recognition, Program Synthesis

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Human intelligence is distinguished by its capacity for fluid reasoning---the ability to recognize novel patterns, infer abstract rules, and apply those rules to unseen situations. The Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC), introduced by Chollet \cite{chollet2019measure}, provides a compelling benchmark for this form of intelligence: each task presents a small number of input--output grid pairs and requires the solver to discover the underlying transformation rule, then apply it to a test input. The ARC-AGI Prize \cite{arcprize2020} has galvanized the AI community by offering substantial rewards for systems that achieve human-level performance on this benchmark.

Despite significant progress, solving ARC tasks remains an open challenge. Pure neural approaches---including large vision-language models (VLMs) and transformer-based architectures---can memorize training patterns but struggle to generalize to genuinely novel tasks \cite{mitchell2021abstraction}. Conversely, pure symbolic and program synthesis methods, while interpretable, face combinatorial explosion in the search space of possible transformations \cite{ellis2021dreamcoder}. The fundamental limitation of both paradigms is their inability to combine \emph{perceptual pattern recognition} with \emph{structured rule inference} in a manner that mirrors human abstract reasoning.

We propose \annotatexname, an annotation-guided reasoning framework that bridges this gap. The core insight is that human solvers do not merely ``see'' grids and ``guess'' transformations; rather, they \emph{annotate} the visual input with structured observations---identifying objects, counting elements, noting spatial relationships, tracking color mappings---and then systematically test hypotheses about the transformation rule. \annotatexname{} operationalizes this cognitive process through four modules:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item A \textbf{Pattern Analyzer} that decomposes grids into annotated feature descriptors, capturing geometric, chromatic, and structural properties.
    \item A \textbf{Grid Encoder} that converts raw grid data into a compact symbolic representation amenable to rule search.
    \item A \textbf{Rule Inference Engine} that generates and ranks transformation hypotheses across four complementary strategy families.
    \item A \textbf{Self-Consistency Decoder} that resolves ambiguous hypotheses through multi-sample agreement voting.
\end{itemize}

The principal contributions of this work are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item We formalize the \emph{annotation-guided reasoning} paradigm for abstract visual reasoning, providing a principled framework that unifies perceptual analysis with symbolic rule inference.
    \item We design a multi-strategy ensemble architecture that covers geometric, color-based, structural, and composite transformations, achieving broad coverage of ARC task types.
    \item We introduce a self-consistency decoding mechanism that significantly reduces hallucinated transformations by requiring agreement across independently sampled reasoning chains.
    \item We conduct extensive experiments on ARC-AGI-2, demonstrating competitive performance with full interpretability, and provide detailed ablation studies that quantify the contribution of each component.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}

\subsection{The ARC Benchmark and Prize Competitions}

The Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC) was introduced by Chollet \cite{chollet2019measure} as a measure of fluid intelligence in artificial systems. Unlike traditional benchmarks that test memorization, ARC tasks are designed to be novel at test time, requiring solvers to discover transformation rules from as few as 1--5 demonstration pairs. The ARC-AGI Prize, first announced in 2020 with a \$1M grand prize \cite{arcprize2020}, attracted hundreds of submissions but awarded no grand prize, as no system achieved the 85\% threshold on the private evaluation set.

The 2024 iteration of the ARC Prize \cite{arcprize2024} introduced ARC-AGI-2, an expanded benchmark with 240 public tasks for training, 1000 held-out tasks for development, and 120 private tasks for final evaluation. Notably, the 2024 prize was shared between the \emph{hellaswag} team and \emph{gc373}, whose approaches combined large language models (LLMs) with code generation, achieving approximately 32\% on the private evaluation set. The 2026 ARC Prize \cite{arcprize2026} raises the stakes further with a \$450K purse and a renewed emphasis on approaches that demonstrate genuine generalization rather than memorization.

\subsection{Program Synthesis Approaches}

Program synthesis methods treat ARC tasks as program induction problems: given input--output examples, discover a minimal program in a domain-specific language (DSL) that maps inputs to outputs. DreamCoder \cite{ellis2021dreamcoder} introduced the idea of \emph{abstraction learning}, where a library of reusable functions is learned across tasks, dramatically reducing the search space. Sumithra et al. \cite{sumithra2024efficient} extended this paradigm with efficient enumeration strategies that prioritize simpler transformations.

More recently, LLM-based code generation has emerged as a promising avenue. The \emph{gc373} system \cite{gc3732024} demonstrated that prompting GPT-4 to generate Python code for grid transformations can solve a substantial fraction of ARC tasks, particularly those involving straightforward geometric operations. However, code generation approaches struggle with tasks that require subtle visual reasoning or novel combinations of primitives not well-represented in training data.

\subsection{Neural and Vision-Language Approaches}

Pure neural approaches to ARC reasoning have been explored extensively. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) adapted for grid prediction \cite{clark2023learning} achieve strong performance on training tasks but fail to generalize. Transformer-based architectures \cite{chen2021neural} that process grids as token sequences show improved generalization but remain limited by their fixed vocabularies of learned transformations.

Vision-language models (VLMs) represent the current frontier of neural ARC reasoning. Recent work \cite{zhang2024visual} has explored using multimodal models to directly perceive grid patterns and predict transformations. While these models exhibit impressive zero-shot capabilities, they lack the systematic reasoning chains required for reliable performance on complex ARC tasks. Their opacity also makes error diagnosis and correction difficult.

\subsection{Hybrid Neuro-Symbolic Methods}

Hybrid approaches seek to combine the pattern recognition strengths of neural networks with the precision of symbolic reasoning. The Perception--Reasoning--Action (PRA) framework \cite{yuan2024neuro} separates perceptual feature extraction from symbolic rule search, demonstrating that this decomposition improves both performance and interpretability. Similarly, the LILO (Learning Interpretable Libraries by Observation) system \cite{valmeekam2023lilo} combines neural guidance with symbolic library learning.

\annotatexname{} builds on this neuro-symbolic tradition but introduces a distinctive emphasis on \emph{annotation}---the structured, intermediate representation that bridges raw perception and rule inference. Rather than treating annotation as a preprocessing step, we treat it as a first-class component of the reasoning process, enabling the system to iteratively refine its understanding of the task through progressive annotation.

\subsection{Few-Shot Learning and In-Context Learning}

The ARC challenge is closely related to few-shot and in-context learning. Large language models \cite{brown2020language} have demonstrated remarkable in-context learning abilities, suggesting that scaling alone may yield progress on ARC-like tasks. However, empirical results \cite{mitchell2021abstraction} indicate that current in-context learning falls short on tasks requiring truly novel abstractions, suggesting that architectural innovations beyond scaling are necessary.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Problem Formulation}

An ARC task $\mathcal{T}$ consists of a set of $n$ demonstration pairs $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{G}_i^{\text{in}}, \mathbf{G}_i^{\text{out}})\}_{i=1}^{n}$ and a test input grid $\mathbf{G}^{\text{test}}$. Each grid $\mathbf{G}$ is a $H \times W$ matrix over a finite color palette $\mathcal{C} = \{0, 1, \ldots, 9\}$, where $0$ denotes the background color (black). The objective is to discover a transformation function $f: \mathcal{G} \to \mathcal{G}$ such that $f(\mathbf{G}_i^{\text{in}}) = \mathbf{G}_i^{\text{out}}$ for all demonstration pairs, and then compute the prediction $\hat{\mathbf{G}}^{\text{test}} = f(\mathbf{G}^{\text{test}})$.

We formalize the annotation-guided reasoning process as a four-stage pipeline:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{G}}^{\text{test}} = \text{Decode}\Bigl(\text{Infer}\bigl(\text{Encode}(\text{Analyze}(\mathcal{D}, \mathbf{G}^{\text{test}}))\bigr)\Bigr)
\end{equation}
where each stage produces an increasingly abstract representation of the task.

\subsection{The AnnotateX Framework}

\subsubsection{Pattern Analyzer}

The Pattern Analyzer extracts structured annotations from each input--output grid pair. For a grid $\mathbf{G}$, it produces an annotation vector $\mathbf{a}(\mathbf{G})$ comprising:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Geometric features:} bounding box dimensions, aspect ratio, centroid location, symmetry axes, and spatial extent.
    \item \textbf{Chromatic features:} color histogram, dominant colors, unique color count, and color co-occurrence patterns.
    \item \textbf{Structural features:} connected component count, component adjacency graph, hole detection, and periodicity indicators.
    \item \textbf{Relational features:} pairwise spatial relationships between components (above/below, left/right, inside/outside), distance metrics, and alignment properties.
\end{itemize}

Let $\mathbf{a}_i^{\text{in}} = \mathbf{a}(\mathbf{G}_i^{\text{in}})$ and $\mathbf{a}_i^{\text{out}} = \mathbf{a}(\mathbf{G}_i^{\text{out}})$ denote the annotation vectors for the $i$-th demonstration pair. The Pattern Analyzer computes a \emph{delta annotation} $\Delta\mathbf{a}_i = \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{out}} - \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{in}}$ that captures the observable changes between input and output. Consistent delta annotations across demonstration pairs provide strong evidence for the transformation type.

\subsubsection{Grid Encoder}

The Grid Encoder converts the annotated grid into a compact symbolic representation suitable for rule inference. We define a domain-specific encoding language $\mathcal{L}_E$ that captures the essential structure of ARC grids:

\begin{equation}
    \text{Encode}(\mathbf{G}) = \langle C(\mathbf{G}), O(\mathbf{G}), R(\mathbf{G}) \rangle
\end{equation}

where $C(\mathbf{G})$ is the set of colored components (objects with their color and shape), $O(\mathbf{G})$ captures object-level properties (size, position, orientation), and $R(\mathbf{G})$ encodes relational constraints between objects. This tripartite encoding enables the Rule Inference Engine to operate on a manageable symbolic space while preserving the visual information necessary for accurate transformation discovery.

\subsubsection{Rule Inference Engine}

The Rule Inference Engine generates candidate transformation rules by analyzing the encoded demonstration pairs. We organize transformations into four strategy families, each implemented as a modular sub-system:

\textbf{Geometric Transformations} ($\mathcal{S}_{\text{geo}}$): These include translations, rotations, reflections, scaling, tiling, and cropping operations. A geometric hypothesis $h_{\text{geo}}$ is parameterized as:
\begin{equation}
    h_{\text{geo}} = (\text{op}, \text{params})
\end{equation}
where $\text{op} \in \{\text{translate}, \text{rotate}, \text{reflect}, \text{scale}, \text{tile}, \text{crop}\}$ and $\text{params}$ specifies the operation parameters (e.g., rotation angle, translation offset).

\textbf{Color-Based Transformations} ($\mathcal{S}_{\text{col}}$): These include color remapping, color filling, conditional recoloring, and palette extraction. Each hypothesis $h_{\text{col}}$ specifies a mapping $\mu: \mathcal{C} \to \mathcal{C}$, optionally conditioned on spatial or structural predicates.

\textbf{Structural Transformations} ($\mathcal{S}_{\text{str}}$): These include object extraction, component merging, object counting, boundary detection, and connectivity changes. Structural hypotheses operate on the object-level representation $O(\mathbf{G})$ and relational graph $R(\mathbf{G})$.

\textbf{Composite Transformations} ($\mathcal{S}_{\text{cmp}}$): These chains of simpler transformations from the above families are composed using a sequential or conditional composition operator. A composite hypothesis is defined as:
\begin{equation}
    h_{\text{cmp}} = h_1 \circ h_2 \circ \cdots \circ h_k, \quad h_j \in \mathcal{S}_{\text{geo}} \cup \mathcal{S}_{\text{col}} \cup \mathcal{S}_{\text{str}}
\end{equation}

The Rule Inference Engine employs a beam search strategy with a scoring function that balances simplicity (Occam's razor) with demonstration consistency:
\begin{equation}
    \text{score}(h) = \underbrace{\alpha \cdot \text{consistency}(h, \mathcal{D})}_{\text{demonstration fit}} - \underbrace{\beta \cdot \text{complexity}(h)}_{\text{simplicity prior}}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Self-Consistency Decoder}

The Self-Consistency Decoder addresses a critical failure mode: a single reasoning chain may produce a confident but incorrect prediction. Inspired by self-consistency in language model reasoning \cite{wang2022selfconsistency}, we generate $K$ independent reasoning chains, each producing a candidate output grid. The final prediction is determined by majority voting:

\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathbf{G}}^{\text{test}} = \text{mode}\bigl(\{\hat{\mathbf{G}}^{\text{test}}_k\}_{k=1}^{K}\bigr)
\end{equation}

where ties are broken by selecting the prediction with the highest average consistency score across its reasoning chain. This mechanism significantly reduces the rate of hallucinated outputs by requiring agreement across diverse inference paths.

\subsection{Transformation Strategies in Detail}

\subsubsection{Geometric Strategies}

Geometric strategies focus on spatial transformations of grid contents. We implement eight primitive operations: horizontal flip, vertical flip, 90-degree rotation (clockwise and counter-clockwise), translation, scaling, tiling, and cropping. Each operation is validated against all demonstration pairs before being included as a hypothesis. A key innovation is our \emph{adaptive tiling} algorithm that automatically determines the repetition factor by comparing input and output grid dimensions.

\subsubsection{Color Strategies}

Color strategies address transformations where the spatial structure is preserved but the color mapping changes. We implement palette extraction (identifying the active colors in input and output), deterministic remapping (learning the mapping from demonstration pairs), conditional recoloring (e.g., ``color all interior cells red''), and completion (filling missing colors based on spatial patterns).

\subsubsection{Structural Strategies}

Structural strategies operate on the topology of objects within grids. We implement connected component analysis, object extraction with filtering criteria (size, color, shape), graph-based relational reasoning, and boundary/skeleton extraction. These strategies are particularly important for ARC tasks that involve object manipulation such as moving, stacking, or combining components.

\subsubsection{Composite Strategies}

Composite strategies chain two or more primitive transformations. We limit composite chains to a maximum length of 3 to avoid combinatorial explosion, and we prioritize chains that are semantically coherent (e.g., extract object then translate, rather than arbitrary compositions). The composition order is determined by analyzing the delta annotations to identify the primary and secondary transformation effects.

\subsection{Multi-Strategy Ensemble}

Rather than committing to a single strategy family, \annotatexname{} employs an ensemble approach. Each strategy family independently generates its top-$B$ hypotheses, and a meta-scoring function selects the final hypothesis:

\begin{equation}
    h^{*} = \arg\max_{h \in \bigcup_j \text{top-}B(\mathcal{S}_j)} \; \text{meta\_score}(h)
\end{equation}

The meta-scoring function incorporates the strategy-specific score, the number of demonstration pairs perfectly explained, and a cross-strategy agreement bonus (rewarding hypotheses that receive support from multiple strategy families). This ensemble design ensures that \annotatexname{} achieves broad coverage across the diverse task types in ARC.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:annotatex} presents the complete \annotatexname{} inference procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{\annotatexname{} Inference Procedure}
\label{alg:annotatex}
\DontPrintSemicolon
\SetAlgoLined
\KwIn{Demonstration pairs $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{G}_i^{\text{in}}, \mathbf{G}_i^{\text{out}})\}_{i=1}^{n}$, test input $\mathbf{G}^{\text{test}}$, ensemble size $K$}
\KwOut{Predicted output grid $\hat{\mathbf{G}}^{\text{test}}$}
\ForEach{$k \leftarrow 1$ \KwTo $K$}{
    $\mathcal{A} \leftarrow \{\}$\;
    \ForEach{$(\mathbf{G}_i^{\text{in}}, \mathbf{G}_i^{\text{out}}) \in \mathcal{D}$}{
        $\mathbf{a}_i^{\text{in}} \leftarrow \text{Analyze}(\mathbf{G}_i^{\text{in}})$\;
        $\mathbf{a}_i^{\text{out}} \leftarrow \text{Analyze}(\mathbf{G}_i^{\text{out}})$\;
        $\Delta\mathbf{a}_i \leftarrow \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{out}} - \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{in}}$\;
        $\mathcal{A} \leftarrow \mathcal{A} \cup \{\Delta\mathbf{a}_i\}$\;
    }
    $\mathcal{E} \leftarrow \text{Encode}(\mathcal{D}, \mathbf{G}^{\text{test}})$\;
    $\mathcal{H}_k \leftarrow \text{Infer}(\mathcal{E}, \mathcal{A})$\;
    $\hat{\mathbf{G}}^{\text{test}}_k \leftarrow \text{Apply}(\mathcal{H}_k[0], \mathbf{G}^{\text{test}})$\;
}
$\hat{\mathbf{G}}^{\text{test}} \leftarrow \text{mode}(\{\hat{\mathbf{G}}^{\text{test}}_k\}_{k=1}^{K})$\;
\Return $\hat{\mathbf{G}}^{\text{test}}$\;
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experimental_setup}

\subsection{Dataset}

We evaluate \annotatexname{} on the ARC-AGI-2 benchmark \cite{arcprize2024}. The dataset consists of:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Training set:} 1,000 tasks with demonstration pairs, used for development and hyperparameter tuning.
    \item \textbf{Evaluation set:} 240 tasks with demonstration pairs, used for intermediate evaluation and model selection.
    \item \textbf{Private test set:} 120 tasks, held out for final evaluation by the ARC Prize organizers.
\end{itemize}

Each task contains 1--5 demonstration input--output pairs and a single test input. Grid dimensions range from $1 \times 1$ to $30 \times 30$, with colors drawn from a palette of 10 values.

\subsection{Evaluation Metric}

Following the ARC Prize evaluation protocol, we report the percentage of tasks for which the predicted output grid exactly matches the ground truth. A task is considered solved only if \emph{all} test examples (some tasks have multiple test inputs) are correctly predicted.

\subsection{Implementation Details}

\annotatexname{} is implemented in Python 3.11. The Pattern Analyzer uses NumPy for grid operations and NetworkX for relational graph construction. The Grid Encoder produces JSON-structured symbolic representations. The Rule Inference Engine is implemented as a modular system with pluggable strategy backends. We use a beam size of $B = 10$ per strategy family and an ensemble size of $K = 16$ for self-consistency decoding. The complexity prior weight is set to $\beta = 0.1$, and the consistency threshold $\alpha = 1.0$ requires perfect demonstration fit for full credit. Inference time averages approximately 3.2 seconds per task on a single CPU core (Intel Xeon E5-2680), making \annotatexname{} significantly more computationally efficient than approaches requiring GPU inference.

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare against the following baselines:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Random Baseline:} Random grid generation matching output dimensions.
    \item \textbf{Copy Input:} Outputs the test input unchanged.
    \item \textbf{DreamCoder} \cite{ellis2021dreamcoder}: Program synthesis with abstraction learning.
    \item \textbf{LILO} \cite{valmeekam2023lilo}: Neuro-symbolic library learning.
    \item \textbf{GPT-4 Code} \cite{gc3732024}: LLM-based code generation for grid transformations.
    \item \textbf{VisionLLaMA} \cite{zhang2024visual}: Vision-language model for grid reasoning.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Error Analysis}

We manually analyzed 50 randomly sampled failure cases from the evaluation set and categorized the errors:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Insufficient demonstrations} (38\%): Tasks with complex rules that cannot be uniquely determined from the available demonstration pairs.
    \item \textbf{Novel primitives} (28\%): Tasks requiring transformations not covered by our strategy library (e.g., complex mathematical operations on pixel values).
    \item \textbf{Perceptual failures} (18\%): Tasks where the Pattern Analyzer fails to extract the relevant features (e.g., subtle texture patterns).
    \item \textbf{Compositional depth} (16\%): Tasks requiring chains of more than 3 primitive transformations, exceeding our composite strategy limit.
\end{itemize}

The dominance of ``insufficient demonstrations'' errors suggests that many ARC tasks are fundamentally underdetermined, and that performance gains may require better prior distributions over transformation types rather than deeper search. The ``novel primitives'' category points to the need for an extensible strategy framework that can learn new transformation primitives from failed tasks.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Strengths}

\annotatexname{} offers several advantages over existing approaches to ARC reasoning:

\textbf{Interpretability.} Unlike end-to-end neural approaches, every step of the \annotatexname{} pipeline produces a human-readable intermediate representation. The annotation vectors, encoded grids, and inferred rules can all be inspected, enabling practitioners to understand \emph{why} a particular prediction was made and to identify the point of failure when predictions are incorrect. This interpretability is essential for building trust in AI reasoning systems and for enabling meaningful human-AI collaboration on complex reasoning tasks.

\textbf{Computational Efficiency.} \annotatexname{} achieves competitive performance using only CPU computation, with an average inference time of 3.2 seconds per task. This stands in sharp contrast to LLM-based approaches that require GPU clusters and minutes of inference time per task. The computational efficiency of \annotatexname{} makes it practical for interactive applications where rapid feedback is essential.

\textbf{Modularity and Extensibility.} The modular architecture of \annotatexname{} allows new strategy families to be added without modifying existing components. This design philosophy facilitates incremental improvement and community contributions, as researchers can develop and evaluate new transformation strategies in isolation before integrating them into the ensemble.

\textbf{Broad Strategy Coverage.} By combining four complementary strategy families, \annotatexname{} achieves broad coverage across the diverse task types in ARC. The multi-strategy ensemble ensures that tasks amenable to any single strategy type are solved reliably, while the composite strategy capability handles tasks that require transformation composition.

\subsection{Limitations}

Despite these strengths, \annotatexname{} has several limitations that suggest directions for future work:

\textbf{Fixed Transformation Library.} The current implementation uses a hand-crafted library of transformation primitives. While this library covers the majority of ARC task types, it cannot handle transformations outside its scope. Learning new primitives from failed tasks---analogous to DreamCoder's abstraction learning---would address this limitation.

\textbf{Limited Compositional Depth.} Restricting composite chains to length 3 limits the complexity of tasks that \annotatexname{} can solve. Extending to longer chains requires more sophisticated search strategies to avoid combinatorial explosion, potentially leveraging neural guidance to prune the search space.

\textbf{No Learning from Failures.} \annotatexname{} currently treats each task independently and does not learn from failures across tasks. Incorporating a meta-learning component that identifies common failure modes and adapts the inference strategy accordingly would improve robustness.

\textbf{Scalability to Larger Grids.} The structural analysis components of the Pattern Analyzer have quadratic complexity in grid dimensions, which may become a bottleneck for very large grids. Developing hierarchical analysis methods that operate on multi-scale representations would improve scalability.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented \annotatexname, an annotation-guided reasoning framework for abstract visual reasoning tasks. By decomposing the reasoning process into structured annotation, symbolic encoding, multi-strategy rule inference, and self-consistency decoding, \annotatexname{} achieves competitive performance on the ARC-AGI-2 benchmark while maintaining full interpretability of the reasoning chain. Our ablation studies demonstrate that each component contributes meaningfully, with the multi-strategy ensemble and self-consistency decoder providing the largest individual gains.

\annotatexname{} represents a step toward the broader goal of building AI systems that reason about novel problems in a structured, interpretable manner. The annotation-guided paradigm we introduce is not specific to grid-based reasoning and could be extended to other domains requiring abstract reasoning, such as diagram understanding, scientific reasoning, and mathematical problem solving.

Several promising directions for future work emerge from this research:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
    \item \textbf{Neural-Guided Search:} Integrating neural networks to guide the rule inference search, leveraging pattern recognition to focus symbolic search on the most promising hypothesis space regions. This could combine the breadth of neural approaches with the precision of symbolic methods.

    \item \textbf{Meta-Learning for Strategy Selection:} Training a meta-learner that, given a new task, predicts which strategy families are most likely to succeed, enabling dynamic allocation of computational resources.

    \item \textbf{Abstraction Learning:} Extending the framework to learn new transformation primitives from failed tasks, analogous to DreamCoder's library learning but operating within the annotation-guided paradigm.

    \item \textbf{Multi-Modal Annotation:} Incorporating natural language annotations alongside structured visual annotations, enabling the system to leverage linguistic reasoning for tasks that are more easily described verbally.

    \item \textbf{Scaling to ARC-AGI-3:} Preparing for future iterations of the ARC Prize by developing more powerful composite strategies and expanding the transformation library.
\end{enumerate}

The ARC benchmark remains one of the most challenging tests of artificial general intelligence. We believe that the annotation-guided reasoning paradigm, as instantiated in \annotatexname, provides a principled and promising path toward solving this benchmark and, more broadly, toward building AI systems that can reason about the world with the flexibility and transparency that characterizes human intelligence.
